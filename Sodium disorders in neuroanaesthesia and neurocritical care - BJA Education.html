          Learning objectives
          By reading this article you should be able to:
          • •
            Explain the principles of sodium homeostasis in neurosurgical and neurocritical care patients.
          • •
            Analyse clinical and biochemical evidence in patients with sodium disorders undergoing neurosurgery or treated in neurocritical care.
          • •
            Monitor and treat dysnatraemia in a timely and safe manner.
          Key points
          • •
            Disorders of sodium balance are common in neurosurgical and neurocritical care patients.
          • •
            Many patients with chronic dysnatraemia are asymptomatic.
          • •
            Dysnatraemia is an important cause of increased morbidity and mortality; this may result from underlying conditions or may be iatrogenic.
          • •
            Treatment of dysnatraemia is complex and correction of sodium balance must occur slowly to avoid complications from acute changes in serum sodium concentration.
          • •
            Dysnatraemia after neurosurgery is usually temporary. Strict fluid balance and daily attention to serum sodium concentration and osmolality are essential.
          Clinical scenario 1
          A patient presented with peripheral visual field defects and a non-functioning pituitary adenoma. Pre-operative serum sodium concentration was normal (135 mmol L-1) with normal thirst and urine output. An uneventful transsphenoidal adenoma resection was performed, but on day 1 the patient developed polyuria with output exceeding 300-500ml in consecutive hours and urine SG of 1.005. Serum sodium concentration increased to 145 mmol litre-1. She was allowed to drink to thirst, but polyuria continued. DDAVP 1μg was administered intravenously. Urine output improved over 24 h, no further Desmopressin was required. Serum sodium concentration returned to normal by day 3.
          On day 5 the patient was ready for discharge, but serum sodium concentration had fallen to 119 mmol L-1. Paired osmolality showed normal urine osmolality, with low serum osmolality of 269 mOsm kg-1. Serum sodium and osmolality continued to fall and the endocrinologists recommended daily fluid restriction to 1500 ml initially and the following day to 750 ml in 24 hours. On day 7, serum sodium fell to 115 mmol L-1 and the patient developed headache, dizziness and myalgia. The patient received i.v. hypertonic saline on the high dependency unit. Urinary sodium remained >20 mmol L-1 throughout and a 9am cortisol level was 503 nmol L-1, thereby excluding adrenal insufficiency. Serum sodium concentration slowly responded to hypertonic saline and fluid restriction; the patient was discharged fifteen days after surgery with a normal serum sodium concentration.
          Clinical scenario 2
          A patient was admitted after a SAH from an anterior communicating artery aneurysmwith hydrocephalus. He had no significant medical history. On admission, blood results showed serum sodium concentration of 136 mmol litre-1, potassium 4.7 mmol L-1, creatinine 62 mmol L-1. The aneurysm was coiled uneventfully on the admission day, Glasgow Coma Score remained at 15 and the patient was noted to be euvolaemic with normal serum sodiumconcentration. He received compound sodium lactate solution intravenously and was encouraged to drink fluids. Discharge to the ward occurred the following day.
          On day 3, the patient was readmitted to the high dependency unit with reduced consciousness level and severe vasospasm on CT angiogram. Treatment commenced with compound sodium lactate solution as maintenance intravenous fluid and noradrenaline to ensure cerebral perfusion. His neurological status improved, but he developed episodes of polyuria. Serum sodium fell to 133 mmol L-1, urine osmolality was low at 94 mOsm kg-1 with a urine sodium < 20 mmol L-1.
          Next day urine output settled, neurology assessment was normal, but noradrenaline continued for vasospasm. Serum sodium concentration was 128 mmol L-1 with urine sodium of 41 mmol L-1, low serum osmolality of 276 mOsm kg-1, high urine osmolality 824 mOsm kg-1 A diagnosis of CSWS was made by the neurocritical care team and intravenous fluids changed to normal saline.
          On day 5 the patient was euvolaemic with polyuria. Serum sodium improved to 132 mmol L-1, urine osmolality was 105 mOsm kg-1 with a urinary sodium of 31mmol L-1. The infusion of normal saline was stopped and compound sodium lactate solution recommenced to maintain fluid balance. A ventriculoperitoneal shunt was inserted; on return to critical care he had high urine outputs with a urine specific gravity of 1.015. Normal saline was then restarted. By day 6 fluid balance was neutral with urine outputs of 200 ml h-1 and serum sodium 136 mmol L-1. Fludrocortisone was commenced and by day 8 serum sodium level was 140 mmol L-1, intravenous fluids ceased as the patient was eating and drinking. Fludrocortisone continued for five days and the patient was discharged to the ward once vasospasm settled.
          Abnormalities of serum sodium concentration (dysnatraemia) are common in patients undergoing neurosurgery and those admitted to critical care with brain injury. Dysnatraemia can present as hyponatraemia or hypernatraemia. Disorders of sodium balance result from underlying systemic disease, traumatic brain injury (TBI), subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH), pituitary surgery and in patients with intracranial tumours. Sodium abnormalities may also result from the inadvertent effects of treatment of these diseases. Abnormal serum sodium concentration, acute or chronic, can increase morbidity and mortality in excess of the underlying pathology. The physiology and diagnosis of dysnatraemia in neurosurgery is complex, but understanding the underlying pathology determines treatment.

          Sodium homeostasis

          Normal serum sodium concentration is 135–145 mmol L−1, determined by an interplay of water and electrolytes, mainly sodium and potassium. In healthy individuals, sodium and water balance are finely regulated by the brain, renal and circulatory systems.
          Sodium is the major extracellular ion and the sodium–potassium ATPase pump at the cell boundary extrudes sodium from the intracellular compartment. Serum sodium concentration is controlled by the addition or removal of water from the extracellular compartment. Changes in serum sodium concentration stimulate the addition or removal of water and therefore controls the extracellular fluid volume.
          Total body sodium is regulated by renal excretion. Sodium is filtered freely by the renal glomerulus and most reabsorbed by the proximal tubules. Reabsorption is controlled by the sympathetic system and atrial (ANP) and brain-type natriuretic peptides (BNP).

          Natriuretic peptides affect the kidney by increasing glomerular filtration rate, causing natriuresis and diuresis. They also reduce renin release, and concentrations of angiotensin II and aldosterone, further increasing sodium excretion and diuresis.
          Serum sodium concentration is the main determinant of serum osmolality, responsible for control and distribution of total body water. Normal serum osmolality is 280–295 mOsm kg−1. This is regulated by sodium control at a renal level, with subsequent changes in water to maintain osmolality. Both sodium and water balance are regulated by serum osmolality. Increased serum osmolality is sensed by the hypothalamic osmoreceptors, producing arginine vasopressin or antidiuretic hormone (ADH). Antidiuretic hormone is released from the posterior pituitary causing reabsorption of water in the kidney's distal tubule and collecting duct, resulting in water retention and concentrated urine. Activation of the renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system (RAAS) causes a net retention of sodium and water. Increased serum osmolality also promotes thirst and increased water consumption in the conscious patient.
          A reduction in plasma volume or arterial pressure stimulates intravascular low-pressure baroreceptors in the right atrium, great veins and high-pressure carotid sinus baroreceptors. Hypovolaemia and hypotension stimulate sympathetic activity and activates the RAAS.

          Sodium dysregulation

          Hyponatraemia

          Perioperative management

          A high index of suspicion for dysnatraemia is required for patients undergoing chemotherapy, radiotherapy, those with pituitary tumours, craniopharyngiomas, or recurrent surgery for brain tumours. Most patients are asymptomatic, and serum electrolyte analysis at the preoperative assessment clinic can be the first indication of sodium impairment. A detailed history often reveals underlying disease or drugs responsible for the sodium disturbance (Table 1, Table 2). Adrenal insufficiency causing hyponatraemia can be fatal and requires urgent treatment.

          It is difficult to identify a specific concentration of serum sodium considered unsafe for a patient to undergo neurosurgery. Each decision will be based on chronicity, the patient's symptoms and the rate of change in serum sodium concentration. In addition, factors including the urgency of surgery, comorbidities, treatment options and patient choice are important. Time permitting, attempts to normalise serum sodium concentration should be made, as dysnatraemia is independently associated with poor outcomes after surgery.

          The patient's age, functional status, urgency of surgery and reversibility of the cause should be used to assess the risk of postponing surgery and correcting electrolyte imbalance, which can itself be driven by the pathological process. Multidisciplinary discussion between neuroanaesthetists, neurologists and neurosurgeons will help to quantify the risks. Hospital admission may be needed to ensure safe correction of serum sodium. Preoperative fluid restriction may be detrimental to outcome, particularly in older people and when major fluid loss is anticipated.
          Neuroanaesthetists must understand potential impacts of surgery and anaesthesia upon fluid balance and serum sodium concentration. One of the most important causes of perioperative dysnatraemia is the iatrogenic use of fluids. Excessive amounts of hypotonic i.v. fluids such as compound sodium lactate can lead to hyponatraemia, whereas repeated use of hypertonic saline or mannitol can cause hypernatraemia.
          Increased urine output may be seen during sellar and suprasellar tumour excision or occasionally during anterior communicating artery aneurysm clipping, if perforating arteries supplying the hypothalamus are damaged. This may reflect evolving cranial diabetes insipidus (CDI), but it is unlikely that serum sodium concentration will change intraoperatively. Treatment other than maintenance of fluid balance is unnecessary and measurement of serum sodium and osmolality can be performed after surgery.
          In the postoperative period dysnatraemia can present immediately or be delayed. Patients with high-grade SAH, anterior circulation aneurysms and hydrocephalus have a high propensity to develop hyponatraemia. Whether the aneurysm is clipped or coiled has no correlation to the incidence of hyponatraemia.

          Hyponatraemia

          Causes and pathophysiology

          Hyponatraemia is often seen with hypotonicity and can sometimes be seen in isotonic and hypertonic conditions. Hypotonic hyponatraemia with a serum osmolality less than 275 mOsm kg−1 has many causes (Table 1, Table 2). Clinical features are caused by cerebral oedema and raised intracranial pressure, as water moves from extracellular to intracellular compartments, caused by differences in effective osmolality between the brain and plasma.
          After brain injury, the most common new causes of hyponatraemia are SIADH and CSWS. Patients may develop adrenal insufficiency with pituitary tumours (50%) and craniopharyngiomas (12–25%).

          Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone

          The pathophysiology of SIADH is not fully understood, but it is thought that hyponatraemia results from renal water reabsorption causing dilutional hyponatraemia in a patient who is clinically euvolaemic . The water retention is secondary to an increased release of ADH.

          Cerebral salt wasting syndrome

          Cerebral salt wasting syndrome, first described in 1950, may result from centrally-mediated increases in renal sodium loss causing hyponatraemia, polyuria and dehydration.

          General management of hyponatraemia

          The cause of hyponatraemia should be identified and underlying conditions treated when possible (Fig 1). For those with brain injury, management is mainly supportive in asymptomatic patients; when changes are acute and the patient is symptomatic, prompt treatment is needed. Increases in serum sodium concentration should not exceed 10 mmol L−1 in 24 h.

In some patients, including older individuals, this may be detrimental as it affects cardiac or renal function. For those with aneurysmal SAH, cerebral perfusion must be maintained and hypotension with fluid restriction is avoided.

          Cerebra salt wasting syndrome

          This syndrome is controversial. It is not a formal diagnosis used by endocrinologists, although it is often referenced within neurosurgery. Hypovolaemia is treated with sodium-containing isotonic fluids such as 0.9% sodium chloride; hypertonic saline may be needed if this is unsuccessful. There is no place for desmopressin (DDAVP) in the treatment of hyponatraemia. Sodium supplementation with salt tablets is occasionally used, but they are poorly tolerated. In cases of refractory hyponatraemia, fludrocortisone is used at a low dose of 0.1 mg, titrated to 0.4 mg daily and weaned slowly.

          Hypernatraemia

          Causes and pathophysiology

          Hypernatraemia is caused by inadequate free water intake or excess water loss, rarely from excessive salt intake. In neurosurgical patients the most common causes are CDI or repeated use of osmotherapeutic agents: mannitol, hypertonic saline or concentrated (8.4%) sodium bicarbonate solution. Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (DI) causes hypernatraemia, but is beyond the scope of this article.
          Diabetes insipidus results in polyuria as the mechanism to concentrate urine is disturbed, causing dehydration, increased serum sodium concentration and hyperosmolality. Cranial diabetes insipidus results from a failure of ADH release from the hypothalamic–pituitary axis, commonly after pituitary surgery, surgery for craniopharyngioma or brain injury. Pituitary stalk transection leads to permanent CDI, but in cases of damage distal to this or to the posterior pituitary lobe, function may recover as ADH can be released from the median eminence.
          When CDI develops after brain injury it usually reflects poor prognosis associated with severe cerebral oedema. Cranial diabetes insipidus is seen after death using neurological criteria and has implications for organ donor management that are outside the scope of this article.

          • Corbett S.
          • Trainor D.
          • Gaffney A.

          Br J Anaesth Educ. 2021; 21: 194-200
          • Google Scholar
          In DI, the patient may be unable to sense or act on thirst or to excrete sodium. This results in hypernatraemia. Some patients after neurosurgery will have reduced consciousness from their brain injury, postoperative cerebral oedema or sedation to facilitate mechanical ventilation. They will be unaware of the sensation of thirst and be unable to increase oral intake. These patients are extremely vulnerable to severe hypernatraemia. Hypernatraemia can result in seizures and coma as it causes a significant osmotic gradient from blood to brain. This results in rapid movement of water from the CNS with cellular dysfunction and lowered seizure threshold.

          General management of hypernatraemia

          In most awake patients, symptoms of thirst, polydipsia and polyuria make the diagnosis of CDI straightforward (Fig 2). It is important to exclude hyperglycaemia and hypercalcaemia. A minority of patients are adipsic; this occurs if simultaneous damage to the hypothalamic thirst receptors causes abnormal thirst regulation. This is manifested as adipsic CDI, characterised by a failure of high plasma osmolality to stimulate ADH secretion and thirst associated with severe hypernatraemia and increased mortality.

          Clinical examination and fluid balance assessment differentiates hypovolaemic hypernatraemia caused by water loss from CDI or dehydration from hypervolaemic hypernatraemia caused by excessive sodium intake. This is particularly important in those with altered conscious levels and reduced or absent symptoms of thirst. Patients with dehydration and normal renal function will have low urine output, and those with DI can have high urine output, in some cases up to 10 L day−1.

          Assessment includes measurement of paired serum and urine osmolalities and electrolytes. Whilst awaiting the results, a urine specific gravity (SG) can be useful. This simple rapid point of care test guides diagnosis; in patients with high urine output, hypernatraemia and urine SG <1.005, a preliminary diagnosis of DI can be made. Rapid laboratory urinary osmolality results are more accurate.
          Treatment of CDI involves the replacement of ADH and correction of water deficit, resulting in a reduction in urine output. Conscious patients should be allowed to drink to thirst, with free access to water, and most will drink enough to regulate sodium and maintain fluid balance. Patients need careful monitoring of fluid balance and serum sodium concentration. For those unable to control oral intake, fluid replacement is maintained with nasogastric water or 5% glucose i. v., correcting blood glucose as needed. If enteral feed is required, low sodium content must be provided. Giving unnecessary i. v. fluids confuses diagnosis and treatment.
          If urine output exceeds 1000 ml in 4 h with low urine SG, small doses of desmopressin are given intranasally or i. v. (Fig 2).

          The third phase may result in permanent DI owing to depletion of ADH reserves. If DI continues beyond the first postoperative week or becomes difficult to manage, the endocrinology team must be contacted.

          Clinical scenarios

          1

          Management principles

          Scenario 1 illustrates common sodium disturbances after pituitary surgery with an initial period of DI, with normal glucose and cortisol concentrations. A further increase in serum sodium concentration on the background of the above would be a trigger for continued treatment with desmopressin. Cranial diabetes insipidus responded to initial management, but the patient then developed SIADH requiring hypertonic saline to correct the acute symptomatic hyponatraemia, unresponsive to fluid restriction. This describes a partial triple phasic response of sodium after pituitary surgery. Fortunately, this patient did not manifest the third phase of permanent DI.
          In scenario 2, a diagnosis of CSWS was made by the critical care team. The patient responded to the initial management with 0.9% saline, but a return to the operating theatre and changes in i.v. fluids decreased his serum sodium concentration. This patient had several episodes of high urine output in the context of a normal SG and hyponatraemia. This is common in patients who have blood pressure augmentation to treat vasospasm. Treatment with fluid restriction in a patient with hyponatraemia and vasospasm is never advised as it can impair neurological outcome.

          Conclusions

          Sodium regulation is complex and is affected by both pathological conditions and neurosurgical intervention. Management of dysnatraemia needs an understanding of pathophysiology with joint working between the endocrinology and neurocritical care teams. Daily monitoring of the patient's clinical condition, and serum sodium and strict fluid balance is needed, as changes can occur within hours. This is particularly important in those with altered conscious levels. Fluid restriction remains the mainstay of treatment in SIADH, fluid and sodium replacement are the choice in CSWS, with glucocorticoids for adrenal insufficiency. Desmopressin is indicated for CDI. Delayed intervention or rapid correction of serum sodium concentrations can result in increased morbidity and mortality.
